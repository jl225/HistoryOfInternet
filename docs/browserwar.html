What was the foundation of it all?

History of the Internet

During the early days of the Internet, before the World Wide Web was created, there were very few web pages and those web pages would consist of nothing but bulky walls of text. However, the next important development in the history of the Internet would soon arrive and change that: Mosaic. Mosaic was a free to download web browser that would propel the Internet’s accessibility to great heights. It was created by Marc Andreesen in 1993 with the intent to make browsing through the internet very easy to use and understand to those who may not have much knowledge on computers. James Clark, the founder of Silicone Graphics, saw a business opportunity with Mosaic and reached out to Andreesen in order to create a new company. However, many did not think that there was any profit to be gained with web browsers, not even Bill Gates, one of the co-founders of the tech giant Microsoft.

Bill Gates’ life goal was to monopolize the computer industry with his Personal Computers (PCs) and Windows software. With Paul Balmer by his side, Gates was doing all he could so that Microsoft would never be outdone by a group of young, eager programmers like Gates was when he first started the company. He would use any means necessary in order to ensure that. Netscape, the company which Clark and Andreseen founded, was tasked with creating a new graphical web browser, one that would propel the Internet commercially and enhance communication over the web. Some Microsoft executives who saw the potential of Netscape attempted to reach out to them in order to learn more about what they were doing. However, the employees at Netscape refused every offer the executives gave. Netscape knew that Microsoft was going to be their biggest competitor.

Navigation, Netscape’s newest browser, was released on December 15th, 1994. Microsoft feared that web browsers would become an alternative platform for softwares, which would leave the Windows Operating System discarded into irrelevance. With Netscape’s success, Bill Gates realized how badly he underestimated the potential of web browsers. In order to rectify his initial blunder, he sent orders to his employees to find ways to innovate what they have, and outmatch what Netscape is able to do. This started the Browser Wars.

Gates sent some Microsoft representatives to meet with Netscape. According to Microsoft employees, they were discussing how the two companies could benefit from each other to create a better user-experience that would allow for profits for both sides. However, according to Netscape employees, the meeting was far less pleasant and involved Microsoft threatening Netscape and offering them a lowly sum of one million dollars for their company. These threats broke Antitrust laws in the United States, and so Netscape took legal action. After only one year as a company, and despite not making any profits yet, Netscape went public with their browser named Navigation, which would lead a large rise in their stock value as well as a large increase in the internet’s popularity. Netscape co-founder Marc Andreseen was caught up in his own success and began to openly gloat about it. He even made some very negative comments towards Bill Gates and Microsoft. This spurred Microsoft's employees and motivated them, leading to their own browser, Internet Explorer.

With their larger pool of financial resources, great programming talent, and some under-handed tactics, Microsoft was able to catch up to Netscape. A large deciding factor in this war was that Microsoft was able to afford to make Internet Explorer a free product, unlike Netscape’s Navigation which had to be paid for to use. By September of 1997, Microsoft officially won the Browser Wars and Netscape would eventually be bought by AOL. Although Netscape lost the Browser War, those involved in the creation of Navigation got millions of dollars from it and so they at least succeeded from a financial standpoint. However, Microsoft would find themselves in legal trouble. In 1998, Congress sued Microsoft. They accused the company of using “predatory and exclusionary” business tactics to put Netscape out of business. The federal Judge ruled Microsoft guilty of these crimes, and wanted Microsoft broken into two seperate companies. However, they took the case to a Court of Appeals and the order to split the company was rescinded. After the case, perhaps with a new perspective on life, Gates gave control of the company to Paul Baulmer and began funding humanitarian work for many causes.
